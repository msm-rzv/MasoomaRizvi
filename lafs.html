Probelm Statement

"The rich tradition of Urdu poetry remains difficult to access online due to fragmented and poorly organized platforms that lack intuitive discovery by author, genre, or theme, hindering meaningful exploration for readers and enthusiasts."

DISCOVERY

I conducted contextual interviews and surveys with 8 users—including literature students, poetry enthusiasts, and casual readers—to understand how they actually think, search, and engage with poetry platforms. Rather than simply asking what they wanted, I observed their behavior through six cognitive dimensions: where their attention naturally goes, how they expect to navigate, what language they use to describe their needs, what mental models they bring from other experiences, what problems they're really trying to solve, and their deeper emotional goals and fears around exploring Urdu poetry.

01

Vision/Attention

  • Users immediately scanned for recognizable poet names (Ghalib, Iqbal)
  • Beautiful Urdu calligraphy drew attention and built trust
  • Cluttered layouts were completely ignored
02

Wayfinding

  • Expected both poet images AND names to be clickable (not just one)
  • Looked for breadcrumbs to know where they were
  • Tried familiar gestures (swipe, hover) from other apps
03

Language

  • Beginners used: "sad poetry," "romantic poems," "spiritual"
  • Students used: poet surnames
  • No platform matched both their vocabulary
04

Memory

  • Brought expectations from both physical poetry books (indexes, collections) AND modern apps (Spotify-like browsing, search bars)
  • Needed design to honor both mental models
05

Decision Making

  • Surface problem: "I can't find specific poets"
  • Actual problem: Overwhelmed by choice, no starting point, unclear how to explore
  • Needed clear paths for different goals (quick sharing vs. deep exploration)
06

Emotion

  • Surface desire: "I want to read poetry"
  • Deeper need: Connect with heritage, process emotions, feel culturally grounded
  • Fear: Appearing ignorant, wasting time on bad platforms, losing their language

User Quotes

Competitive Analysis

A competitive analysis of existing Urdu poetry platforms revealed several failures across multiple cognitive dimensions:


  1. Vision/Attention: Cluttered layouts, poor Urdu typography, no visual hierarchy
  2. Wayfinding: Unclear navigation, no breadcrumbs, inconsistent interactions
  3. Language: Assumed expertise, inconsistent terminology
  4. Memory: Violated modern web expectations, outdated design
  5. Decision Making: No clear starting points, overwhelming options
  6. Emotion: Made users feel stupid, frustrated rather than inspired

These insights reinforced the need for a platform like Lafs, which prioritizes simplicity, organization, and user engagement.

DEFINE

01

Appeal

    What draws the user in immediately
  • Beautiful Urdu calligraphy and clean design
  • Accessible language matching what they actually say
  • Clear starting points for exploration
02

Enhance

What solves their real problems
  • Intuitive navigation with familiar patterns
  • Multiple ways to browse (by poet, genre, search)
  • Mobile-responsive, fast, reliable
03

Awaken

    What connects them to their deeper goals
  • Honors cultural heritage through design
  • Builds confidence, not intimidation
  • Creates sense of belonging to literary tradition

Goals

  1. Create a centralized hub for Urdu poetry that is easy to navigate and explore.
  2. Organize poetry by poet, genre, and theme to enhance discoverability.
  3. Design a visually appealing and user-friendly interface that appeals to a global audience.
  4. Promote Urdu poetry by making it accessible and engaging for both seasoned readers and newcomers.

Mission Statement

"To celebrate and preserve the rich heritage of Urdu poetry by providing a centralized and beautifully curated platform that makes exploration intuitive and welcoming for literature enthusiasts and new audiences alike."

Early Testing & Iterations

Testing the low-fidelity prototype revealed important usability issues. The homepage was overcrowded with repeated call-to-action buttons. Additionally, the "Poetry" navigation label confused users—they expected a general library rather than genre-based browsing as intended. I addressed these issues by simplifying the homepage layout, removing redundant CTAs, and renaming the "Poetry" label to "Genre" clearing up any confusion.

Brand & Visual Identity

The name Lafs (لفظ) translates to "words" in Urdu—short, meaningful, and culturally resonant. The logo uses Urdu script for immediate cultural authenticity, while a delicate feather pen icon reinforces the poetry and writing tradition. The color palette of deep reds, golds, grays, blacks, and dark blues creates a classic, elegant look that evokes the timeless quality of Urdu poetry. Clean, high-contrast typography ensures readability of Urdu script, while subtle Urdu letters above navigation items (matching initial phonetic sounds like 'ر' above "Writers") add cultural layering without confusion.

DELIVER

Usability Testing

After building the high-fidelity prototype of LAFS, I conducted informal usability testing with 4 users from my intended audience:

  • 1 poetry enthusiast familiar with South Asian literature
  • 1 Urdu reader with intermediate proficiency
  • 2 general users interested in exploring poetry in translation

Goals of Testing

  • Test navigation clarity and discoverability of poet pages
  • Evaluate how users interact with the dual-language presentation
  • Assess the overall browsing flow and visual hierarchy

Key Insights

"The design feels elegant and respectful of the poetry."

"I didn't realize the homepage slider was clickable at first."

"I kept clicking on the poet name thinking it would go to their page."

Final Iterations

  • Slider clickability: Added hover effects and cursor changes to indicate interactivity
  • Poet name links: Made both images and names clickable to improve discoverability
