SUKOON

JUNE 2025 - DECEMBER 2025

"Sukoon bridges the gap between mental health and spirituality. It's an interactive emotional tool rooted in the Quran: users select an emotion—gratitude, grief, hope, or anxiety—and receive a handpicked ayah and tafsir for mindful reflection and inner calm."

GLOSSARY

Quran: The central religious text of Islam.
Ayah: A verse from the Quran.
Tafsir: Explanation or interpretation of a verse.
Emotion-to-Ayah Matching: Linking feelings to verses for spiritual insight.

Problem Statement

"Mental health tools and Quran apps often exist separately. Sukoon brings them together—offering spiritual grounding for emotional states through Quranic ayahs and thoughtful tafsir."

DISCOVERY

I surveyed and interviewed individuals from Muslim communities to explore how people process emotions spiritually. Many shared the struggle of feeling mentally overwhelmed while seeking spiritual clarity. Most existing tools were either purely clinical or strictly religious, with little overlap. Rather than simply asking what they wanted, I observed their behavior through six cognitive dimensions: where their attention naturally goes, how they expect to navigate, what language they use to describe their needs, what mental models they bring from other experiences, what problems they're really trying to solve, and their deeper emotional goals and fears around exploring spiritual resources for emotional wellbeing.

01

Vision/Attention

  • Users were immediately drawn to calming visuals and emotion-based categories
  • Celestial imagery and glowing orbs created instant spiritual connection
  • Overwhelming text or multiple options were quickly dismissed
02

Wayfinding

  • Expected clear indication of where they were in the emotional journey
  • Needed easy way to return to emotion selection
  • Looked for familiar navigation patterns (back buttons, labels)
03

Language

  • Used simple emotional terms: "anxious," "grateful," "hopeful," "grieving"
  • Preferred clear explanations over religious jargon
  • Wanted context (tafsir) in accessible language
04

Memory

  • Brought expectations from meditation apps (calm, focused experiences)
  • Also expected features from Quran apps (verse display, translations)
  • Needed design that honored both mental models
05

Decision Making

  • Surface problem: "I can't find relevant verses for how I feel"
  • Actual problem: Overwhelmed searching through entire Quran, unclear where to start
  • Needed curated, emotion-specific guidance with option to explore more
06

Emotion

  • Surface desire: "I want spiritual guidance"
  • Deeper need: Find peace, feel seen by something greater, process difficult emotions
  • Fear: Being judged for not knowing enough Quran, feeling disconnected from faith

User Quotes

Competitive Analysis

A review of Quran and mental health apps revealed critical failures across multiple cognitive dimensions:


  1. Vision/Attention: Cluttered interfaces, no emotional categorization
  2. Wayfinding: Complex navigation, unclear user location
  3. Language: Academic or overly clinical terminology
  4. Memory: Violated expectations from both meditation and Quran apps
  5. Decision Making: No clear starting points for emotional exploration
  6. Emotion: Made users feel inadequate rather than supported

Sukoon emerged as a gentle bridge—calm, focused, and spiritually rooted—helping users access divine wisdom when they need it most.

Key Pain Points Identified

  1. Difficult to find ayahs that match emotional states
  2. Lack of accessible, simple tafsir
  3. Existing platforms lack intentional emotional UX

Goals

  1. Create a calm, focused tool that helps users connect with the Quran through their feelings
  2. Pair each ayah with handpicked tafsir for emotional depth
  3. Design a minimal, inviting interface that encourages reflection

Mission Statement

"To create an emotionally-responsive digital space where users can turn to the Quran in moments of joy, pain, or uncertainty—and find immediate spiritual comfort, bridging mental health and faith."

Early Testing & Iterations

Testing the low-fidelity prototype revealed important wayfinding issues. Once a user had generated an ayah, they struggled to understand exactly where they were in the journey and how to navigate back. I addressed these issues by adding an emotion label at the bottom of each generation page to clearly indicate their current location, and implemented a back button so users could easily return to the main emotion selection page.

Brand & Visual Identity

The name Sukoon, meaning "tranquility" in Arabic, was chosen to reflect the app's calming intent. It represents the peace we seek through divine words. The branding draws from soft earthy tones and flowing typography to soothe the user from the moment they land on the page.Sukoon's visual identity is built around the idea of spiritual calm and emotional resonance. The logo is a soft, glowing orb—an abstract representation of the spirit, capturing the essence of the soul. Each emotion—gratitude, grief, hope, anxiety—is symbolized by its own glowing circle, subtly animated to reflect the fluid and shifting nature of inner states. The backdrop of stars and interstellar imagery evokes a sense of vastness beyond the material world—a reminder that peace and guidance can come from something greater, something unseen. The design leans into a celestial aesthetic to reflect the Quranic idea of calm as a gift from the Divine: weightless, light-filled, and otherworldly. The color palette is composed of layered pinks and soft orange, evoking warmth, compassion, and gentle reflection.

DELIVER

Usability Testing

After building the high-fidelity prototype of Sukoon, I conducted informal usability testing with 4 users from my intended audience:

  • 1 Islamic studies student
  • 1 daily Quran reader
  • 2 general users seeking emotional or spiritual reflection

Goals of Testing

  • Test emotional-to-ayah connection for clarity and relevance
  • Understand how users interpret and engage with the tafsir section
  • Evaluate the calmness and flow of the overall experience

Key Insights

"The orb visuals are really soothing, it makes it feel spiritual."

"I didn't know the tafsir button was a button at first."

"It's peaceful and simple, nothing too overwhelming."

Final Iterations

  • Tafsir and Refresh button clarity: Styled buttons more clearly to feel interactive and intentional
  • Visual pacing: Kept visual pacing slow and intuitive, reinforcing the emotion-focused, reflective nature of the tool
