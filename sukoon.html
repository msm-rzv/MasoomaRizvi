SUKOON

JANUARY 2025 – MARCH 2025

"Sukoon bridges the gap between mental health and spirituality. It’s an interactive emotional tool rooted in the Quran: users select an emotion—gratitude, grief, hope, or anxiety—and receive a handpicked ayah and tafsir for mindful reflection and inner calm."

GLOSSARY

Quran: The central religious text of Islam.
Ayah: A verse from the Quran.
Tafsir: Explanation or interpretation of a verse.
Emotion-to-Ayah Matching: Linking feelings to verses for spiritual insight.
بِسْمِ ٱللّٰهِ ٱلرَّحْمَٰنِ ٱلرَّحِيمِ (Bismillah al-Rahman al-Raheem): Arabic for "In the name of God, the Most Compassionate, the Most Merciful." Often said before beginning something important, and is at the beginning of almost every chapter of the Quran. It appears at the top of each page on Sukoon as a way to start with peace and divine remembrance.

"Mental health tools and Quran apps often exist separately. Sukoon brings them together—offering spiritual grounding for emotional states through Quranic ayahs and thoughtful tafsir."

DISCOVERY

I surveyed and interviewed individuals from Muslim communities to explore how people process emotions spiritually. Many shared the struggle of feeling mentally overwhelmed while seeking spiritual clarity. Most existing tools were either purely clinical or strictly religious, with little overlap.

“Sometimes I just want one ayah that speaks to how I feel, not a whole lecture.” – Fatima, 19

“Reading the tafsir adds so much more context, but I don’t know where to start.” – Ahmed, 27

“I feel overwhelmed searching for verses that match what I’m going through.” – Mariam, 21

Competitive Analysis

A review of Quran and mental health apps revealed a critical gap:

  1. Few resources intentionally bridge emotional well-being with spiritual reflection.
  2. Quran apps are feature-heavy but not emotionally intuitive.
  3. Mental health tools rarely incorporate Islamic or Quranic frameworks.

Sukoon emerged as a gentle bridge—calm, focused, and spiritually rooted—helping users access divine wisdom when they need it most.

DEFINE

Key Pain Points Identified:

  1. Difficult to find ayahs that match emotional states.
  2. Lack of accessible, simple tafsir.
  3. Existing platforms lack intentional emotional UX.

Goals:

  1. Create a calm, focused tool that helps users connect with the Quran through their feelings.
  2. Pair each ayah with handpicked tafsir for emotional depth.
  3. Design a minimal, inviting interface that encourages reflection.

Mission Statement

"To create an emotionally-responsive digital space where users can turn to the Quran in moments of joy, pain, or uncertainty—and find immediate spiritual comfort, bridging mental health and faith."

Design & Branding Decisions

Name Selection

The name Sukoon, meaning “tranquility” in Arabic, was chosen to reflect the app’s calming intent. It represents the peace we seek through divine words. The branding draws from soft earthy tones and flowing typography to soothe the user from the moment they land on the page.

Visual Identity

Sukoon's visual identity is built around the idea of spiritual calm and emotional resonance. The logo is a soft, glowing orb—an abstract representation of the spirit, capturing the essence of the soul. Each emotion—gratitude, grief, hope, anxiety—is symbolized by its own glowing circle, subtly animated to reflect the fluid and shifting nature of inner states. The backdrop of stars and interstellar imagery evokes a sense of vastness beyond the material world—a reminder that peace and guidance can come from something greater, something unseen. The design leans into a celestial aesthetic to reflect the Quranic idea of calm as a gift from the Divine: weightless, light-filled, and otherworldly.

Color Palette

The Sukoon color palette is built on soft neutrals, green for peace, and midnight blue for reflection. These tones guide the emotional experience of the user and mirror the spiritual theme of the Quranic verses.

DELIVER

After building the high-fidelity prototype of Sukoon, I conducted informal usability testing with 4 users from my intended audience:

  • 1 Islamic studies student
  • 1 daily Quran reader
  • 2 general users seeking emotional or spiritual reflection

Goals of Testing

  • Test emotional-to-ayah connection for clarity and relevance
  • Understand how users interpret and engage with the tafsir section
  • Evaluate the calmness and flow of the overall experience

Key Feedback + Iterations

“The orb visuals are really soothing, it makes it feel spiritual.”

“I didn't know the tafsir button was a button at first.”

“It’s peaceful and simple, nothing too overwhelming.”

“It’s hard to read tafsir on mobile — maybe adjust the spacing?”

Usability Testing

Based on this feedback, the following changes were made:

  • Styled the Tafsir and Refresh buttons more clearly to feel interactive and intentional.
  • Improved spacing and layout of the tafsir section for better readability, especially on mobile screens.
  • Reduced hover-based interactions and added gentle transitions to support a calming, focused experience.
  • Adjusted layout responsiveness to ensure the interface remains fluid across screen sizes.
  • Kept visual pacing slow and intuitive, reinforcing the emotion-focused, reflective nature of the tool.
